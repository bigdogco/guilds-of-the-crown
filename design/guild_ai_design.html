Foundation

Core architecture — noticeboard pattern

The spine that all other systems plug into. A blackboard pattern combined with utility-based task selection, similar to Dwarf Fortress and RimWorld.

The key insight: producers don't know about consumers, and consumers don't know about producers. They both just talk to noticeboards. This is what lets you scale from 20 NPCs to 200 without rewriting anything.

The Task as a data contract

The most important design decision is what a Task actually contains. Everything else flows from this.

Task:
  id, type, category
  postedBy, claimedBy, targetEntity, location
  basePriority, rewardGold, payload
  expiresAt, minSkill[], requiredTraits, preferredTraits
  state: Posted | Claimed | InProgress | Done | Failed | Expired

The key: the task describes the job, not who should do it. Matching happens at claim-time via utility scoring. A guard task doesn't say "guard NPC required" — it says "requires Combat skill ≥ 3, prefers Brave trait." Any NPC who qualifies can consider it. This means you can add new NPC archetypes later without touching the task posters.

Scoped noticeboards — not one global list

Don't maintain one giant list and make every NPC scan it every tick. Instead, maintain several scoped pools:

  • City board — posted by CityAiSystem, visible to unemployed townspeople, guards, clerks
  • Workshop board (per business) — visible only to employees of that workshop
  • Regional gather board — visible to gatherers, lumberjacks, hunters
  • Trade caravan board — long-haul tasks for merchants and hauliers
  • Personal needs board — one per NPC, their own queue of hunger/sleep/socialise tasks

NPCs have a TaskSubscriptions component listing which boards to check and in what order. A workshop employee checks: personal needs (urgent) → their workshop board → fallback to city board if unemployed. This gives you the "guard checks guard tasks first" behaviour cheaply.

Task selection: utility scoring

When an NPC becomes idle, they score candidates with a utility function:

utility = basePriority
        × traitAffinity(npc, task)
        × skillFit(npc, task)
        × (1 / (1 + distance × distanceWeight))
        × rewardFactor(npc.greed, task.reward)
        × needsModifier(npc.needs)

Each NPC's traits and attributes reshape the utility curve, which is how personality-driven emergence happens for free. A Greedy NPC weights reward heavily. A Loyal NPC has a high multiplier on employer tasks. A Lazy NPC picks nearby tasks. A Hungry NPC has work utility crushed until they eat.

Performance tip

Don't score every task every tick. Only re-score when (a) a new task is posted to a subscribed board, (b) the NPC finishes their current task, or (c) a major event flag flips (needs critical, employer changed). Use signal handlers on Task construction to dirty-flag subscribed NPCs.

How economic systems plug in

  • CityAiSystem — posts BuyFromMarket, GuardPatrol, EmergencyImport tasks on schedule
  • WorkshopSystem — posts FetchResource and CraftRecipe tasks to each workshop's board
  • MarketSystem — mostly reactive (holds price engine), posts Restock hints when stock is low
  • RivalAiSystem — each rival is a BusinessBrain entity posting to its own workshop boards
  • NpcNeedSystem — decays needs, posts personal tasks when thresholds crossed

The economic loop now drives itself: NPCs get hungry → they buy bread → bakery revenue rises → baker posts craft tasks → employees gather wheat → wheat demand rises → farmers prosper. No system needs to know about any other — they all communicate through noticeboards and the market.

System tick order

  1. NeedsSystem — decay needs, post personal tasks
  2. CityAiSystem — check stocks, post city tasks
  3. WorkshopSystem + RivalAiSystem — check inventories, post workshop tasks
  4. MarketSystem — update prices, post restock hints
  5. NpcTaskSelectionSystem — idle NPCs score and claim
  6. NpcActionSystem — NPCs execute claimed tasks
  7. FinanceSystem — settle real-time revenue events
  8. EndOfDaySystem (daily) — wages, upkeep, taxes, loan repayments
NPC Identity

Trait system

Data-driven personality system that reshapes utility scoring. Traits are separate from attributes and skills — each plugs into behaviour differently.

Three distinct concepts

  • Attributes — Strength, Intellect, Charisma, Dexterity, Constitution. Rarely change. Modify execution (faster craft, more carry weight).
  • Skills — Smithing, Baking, Combat, Haggling, Gathering, Literacy. Grow through doing, modulated by attributes. Modify quality and speed.
  • Traits — Greedy, Loyal, Brave, Lazy, Honest, Ambitious. Rarely change. An NPC has 2-4 at any time. Modify decision-making.

Core vs acquired traits

Starting traits (3-5) are the NPC's core identity from background at spawn. Unlikely to ever be lost. These are "who this person is."

Acquired traits sit on top, earned through life events. More volatile — can be lost if conditions reverse. These are "what this person has lived through." Trait cap: 20 total.

Example

Hilda (Blacksmith's daughter)
Core: Hardworking, Proud, Loyal
Acquired: Battle-scarred, Bitter

Traits as tagged utility modifiers

Instead of hardcoding trait behaviour in the scoring function, each trait declares how it bends the utility formula. New traits are just new data.

TraitDef:
  id, name, description
  modifiers: list of UtilityModifier
  incompatibleWith: list of TraitId
  synergyWith: list of TraitId

UtilityModifier:
  target (RewardWeight, DistanceWeight, LoyaltyBonus, etc.)
  filter (category, tag, poster)
  multiplier, addend

Example trait definitions

Greedy
  reward_weight × 2.0 (any task)
  loyalty_bonus × 0.3 (any task)
  incompatibleWith: Generous, Ascetic

Loyal
  loyalty_bonus × 3.0 (poster = employer)
  reward_weight × 0.5 (poster ≠ employer)
  incompatibleWith: Fickle, Mercenary

Lazy
  distance_weight × 2.5 (any task)
  base_priority −0.1 (any task)
  incompatibleWith: Industrious

Ambitious
  skill_growth_weight × 1.8 (any task)
  reward_weight × 1.5 (tag = promotion_path)

Brave
  risk_tolerance +0.5
  base_priority × 1.4 (category = combat)
  incompatibleWith: Cowardly

Where traits come from

At spawn — roll from a weighted table based on background. A mining town produces more Brave/Tough workers; a merchant city more Shrewd/Worldly ones. Town character emerges naturally.

Earned through play — traits can be gained (rarely) from sustained conditions. An NPC underpaid for months may gain Bitter. An NPC who survives combat may gain Veteran. Most trait gains should be conditional on sustained behaviour, not single events.

TraitGainRule (Bitter):
  trigger: underpaid X days in a row, OR
           wage_request rejected Y times
  chance_per_check: 0.15
  check_frequency: weekly
  prerequisites: not has(Content), not has(Cheerful)
  erodes: Loyal, Content, Trusting

Trait loss rules

Loss rules are stricter than gain rules — traits should be sticky once earned. An NPC who became Bitter during a rough patch might take a full season of good treatment to shed it. Core traits should be essentially unlosable.

Suggested starter set (~20)

  • Work ethic — Hardworking, Lazy, Ambitious, Complacent
  • Money — Greedy, Generous, Frugal
  • Loyalty — Loyal, Fickle, Opportunistic
  • Courage — Brave, Cowardly
  • Social — Charming, Gruff, Honest, Deceitful
  • Life experience (acquired) — Veteran, Bitter, Drunkard, Pious
Failure mode to watch for

Trait effects getting swamped by base utility math. When tuning, first get the base utility reasonable without traits, then add traits with larger-than-intuitive multipliers (1.5x-3x, not 1.1x). Trait presence should visibly shift decisions. Players should say "my Loyal blacksmith stayed when the rival offered more" and actually notice it.

Competition

Rival AI

NPC-run businesses that compete with the player using the same systems. No special cases, no scripting — rivals are just player-businesses driven by a RivalBrain decision layer.

Core principle: rivals are player-businesses with an AI driver

A rival business is an entity with the exact same components as a player business: workshop, employees, inventory, finances, market presence. The only difference is that instead of a human making decisions, a RivalBrain component drives it.

  • Rivals use your WorkshopSystem, MarketSystem, FinanceSystem identically
  • They post to the same noticeboards
  • They hire from the same NPC pool
  • They buy and sell on the same market
  • They can go bankrupt and be replaced, just like the player can

What a rival needs to decide

  1. Production — what to craft, in what quantity, using which ingredients (daily)
  2. Sourcing — where to buy raw materials (daily)
  3. Hiring/firing — add workers, respond to wage demands (weekly)
  4. Wages — accept or reject wage-rise requests (weekly)
  5. Reinvestment — upgrade workshop, buy extraction building, expand (monthly/seasonal)
  6. Competition — respond to rivals (including player) — underbid, outproduce, coexist

Strategic archetypes

Different rivals feel different through strategic archetypes — high-level business personalities that shape how the RivalBrain weights its decisions.

Volume Producer

Goal: dominate through sheer output. Buys extraction buildings early, maximises worker count, cheap raw materials. Medium risk. Undercuts on volume when rivals appear.

Quality Specialist

Goal: premium goods, highest skill tier. Hires skilled workers, upgrades workshop tier, slow careful growth. Low risk. Ignores cheap rivals, targets the high end.

Opportunist

Goal: chase whatever's most profitable right now. Flexibility, cash reserves, cheap workers. High risk. Pivots trades if a market gets crowded, starts a second business earlier.

Steady Merchant

Goal: reliable modest profit, don't rock the boat. Stable employees, modest reinvestment, avoid debt. Very low risk. Coexists, rarely escalates.

The Aggressor (villain archetype, use sparingly)

Goal: dominate the market, push rivals out. Aggressive expansion, undercutting, poaching competitors' workers. High risk. Will run at a loss temporarily to bankrupt rivals. One per town max.

Archetype as weight vector

Archetypes are data, not code. The RivalBrain has one decision function; each archetype is a set of parameters:

Volume Producer:
  production_weights:
    output_per_day: 1.5
    quality: 0.5
    variety: 0.3
  sourcing_weights:
    price: 1.8
    reliability: 0.6
    own_supply_preference: 1.4
  hiring_weights:
    worker_count_target: max
    skill_requirement: 0.4
    wage_sensitivity: 1.5
  reinvestment_weights:
    cash_reserve_days: 10
    extraction_building: 2.0
    worker_slots: 1.8
    workshop_upgrade: 0.7
    second_trade: 0.5
  competition_weights:
    aggression: 0.7
    dumping_threshold: 0.6
    price_race_willingness: 0.8

The rival decision loop

RivalBrain.tick(rival, tick_type):

  if tick_type == DAILY:
    assess_production(rival)
    assess_sourcing(rival)
    post_production_tasks(rival)
    post_gather_tasks(rival)

  if tick_type == WEEKLY:
    review_employees(rival)
    handle_wage_requests(rival)
    consider_hiring(rival)
    assess_competition(rival)

  if tick_type == MONTHLY:
    review_financial_health(rival)
    consider_reinvestment(rival)
    review_strategic_posture(rival)

Competition — how rivals notice each other

Rivals observe the market the same way the player does:

assess_competition(rival):
  my_share = rival.listed_stock / Market.total_stock
  price_trend = Market.price_trend(last_14_days)

  // detect dumping
  if price_trend.dropped_by > 0.3:
    rival.stress_level += 0.2
    if price_race_willingness > 0.5:
      dump_inventory(aggression)
    else:
      throttle_production(factor = 0.5)

  // detect new rival
  if Market.new_entrants(last_30_days) > 0:
    log_event("new competitor noticed")
    if aggression > 0.8:
      post_poaching_tasks(target = new_entrant)

  // detect player growing
  if player.trade == rival.trade and player.revenue_trend > 0.5:
    if archetype == Aggressor:
      escalate_against_player(rival)

Rivals react to what they can see: stock levels, price swings, new businesses appearing. They don't read the player's bank balance or intentions. This keeps them fair.

Rival lifecycle

Rivals should be mortal. A town shouldn't have the same three families forever — it should feel like a living economy.

  • Spawning — triggered when a trade becomes profitable, or on slow schedule, or as succession when a rival goes bankrupt (former employee inherits archetype loosely based on experience)
  • Decline — uses existing bankruptcy system. When entering grace period, RivalBrain switches to "desperation mode" — slash expenses, liquidate stock
  • Bankruptcy — entity removed, workers return to NPC pool. Player gets notification — a rival falling is a reward.

Making rivals feel fair (not cheating)

  1. Rivals use the same market prices as the player
  2. Rivals pay the same wages the player would
  3. Rivals can fail — visibly, with notifications
  4. Rivals don't see into player's inventory or finances — only market-visible info
  5. Rivals' decisions should be visible in their actions (dumping = price drop player notices)

One acceptable "cheat": spawning capital. A new rival needs starting gold to exist. That's worldbuilding. After spawn, they play by the same rules.

Elegant property

From another rival's perspective, the player is just another rival. The assess_competition function treats the player identically. If the player becomes dominant, Aggressors push back. If the player is small, they're ignored. No special "challenge the player" code needed.

Coordination

Task contention & teams

Where noticeboards stay clean or turn to spaghetti. Handling race conditions, greedy locks, priority inversion, and multi-NPC tasks.

The three contention problems

  1. Race conditions — two NPCs claim the same task simultaneously
  2. Greedy locking — one NPC claims too many tasks, blocking others
  3. Priority inversion — low-priority task claimed by wrong NPC, high-priority task sits unclaimed

Plus a harder structural problem: multi-NPC tasks (hauling heavy carts, raids, staffing counters).

Claiming: atomic, with a short commitment

Tasks are exclusive once claimed, and claiming is atomic:

claim_task(npc, task):
  if task.state != Posted:
    return false
  task.state = Claimed
  task.claimedBy = npc
  task.claimedAt = now
  emit TaskClaimedEvent(task, npc)
  return true

Single-threaded task selection means no race conditions. While an NPC is scoring a task, another might claim it first — that's fine, the first NPC's score is just stale and they'll re-score next pass.

Claim-then-reserve, not claim-then-work

Add a reservation timeout. When a task moves to Claimed, it has a deadline to transition to InProgress. If the deadline passes, the task auto-reverts to Posted.

check_claim_timeouts():
  for task in all_tasks where state == Claimed:
    if now - task.claimedAt > CLAIM_TIMEOUT:
      release_task(task)

release_task(task):
  prior_claimer = task.claimedBy
  task.state = Posted
  task.claimedBy = null
  prior_claimer.reliability -= 0.05  // optional penalty

Short timeouts (1-2 in-game hours local, ~1 day for long-haul). Combined with reliability feeding back into utility, flaky NPCs get scored lower over time — self-correcting.

Preventing greedy locking

Rule: one active task per NPC at a time. Optional exception: queuing one "next up" task when current is near done, preventing idle gaps.

can_queue_next_task(npc):
  return npc.currentTask != null
     and npc.currentTask.estimatedCompletion < 5_minutes
     and npc.queuedTask == null

Priority inversion — the routing problem

Master smith Aldric's apprentice Finn claims a trivial "craft horseshoe" task. Then a Legendary Sword commission arrives (needs Smithing 8+). Finn is locked, Aldric should have been freed for the legendary work.

Approach A: task-first matching (for urgent tasks)

High-priority tasks actively solicit the best candidate:

post_high_priority_task(task):
  noticeboard.add(task)
  if task.basePriority > SOLICIT_THRESHOLD:
    candidates = find_idle_npcs_matching(task)
    best = argmax(candidates, score(npc, task))
    if best != null:
      offer_task(best, task)

"Push" for urgent work, "pull" for everything else.

Approach B: NPC tiering within a board (for workshops)

board_selection_tick():
  for tier in [Master, Journeyman, Apprentice, Idle]:
    for npc in idle_npcs_in_tier(tier):
      task = score_and_claim_best(npc, board)

Use Approach A for city-level urgent tasks, Approach B for workshops where hierarchy is clear.

Staleness and re-scoring

  • Before InProgress: NPC can drop current task if alternative is ~30% better utility
  • Once InProgress: NPC commits unless critical need (starving, under attack)

This gives stable behaviour with flexibility for real emergencies. Task-switching mid-work looks jittery ("why did my worker just walk away from the forge?").

Multi-NPC tasks (teams)

Some tasks need multiple NPCs: hauling heavy carts, raiding warehouses, staffing the shop counter, manning guard towers. Use a TeamTask that holds multiple role slots:

TeamTask:
  (everything a normal Task has, plus:)
  roles: list of RoleSlot
  formed: bool
  start_when_filled: bool
  timeout_if_unfilled: duration

RoleSlot:
  role_name: "Guard" | "Hauler" | "Lookout"
  requirements: skill + trait filters
  claimedBy: entity (null if open)

NPCs claim a role within a team task. Scoring picks the best-matching open role. When all roles fill, the team forms and activates.

The gathering problem

Start with "Rally and wait" with a short formation timeout (~30 in-game min). Claimers move to rendezvous and idle. If team doesn't form in time, disband and release NPCs.

Preventing team starvation

  • Minimum viable size — "requires 3-5, starts at 3"
  • Substitution rules — Guard slot can accept a Brave peasant at reduced effectiveness
  • Auto-disband — if unfilled too long AND no matching idle NPCs exist

Team rewards

  • Equal split (simple)
  • Role-weighted (Captain 40%, Guards 20% each)
  • Skill-weighted

This creates a lever for Greedy NPCs — they prefer tasks where they expect bigger shares.

Worked example: a workshop in motion

Setup: smithy has Aldric (master, Smithing 7), Finn (apprentice, Smithing 2), Greta (gatherer). Inventory: 2 iron, 0 coal.

Tick: WorkshopSystem inspects inventory, posts:

  • Task A: CraftRecipe(sword) — needs 2 iron, 1 coal. Blocked. Stays WaitingForIngredients
  • Task B: FetchResource(coal, 3) — urgent
  • Task C: CraftRecipe(nail_batch) — ready
  • Task D: RepairTools — low priority

NPCs score:

  • Greta scores B highest (gather, specialty) → claims B
  • Finn scores C highest (matches low skill) → claims C
  • Aldric scored C too, but Finn claimed first; re-scores → D

Half-hour later: Greta returns with coal. Task A unblocks, flips to Posted. Aldric finishes D → claims A. Forge action begins. Finn finishes C, nothing matches, enters idle. NpcNeedSystem posts Eat to Finn's personal board.

What happened

No central orchestrator decided who does what. Each NPC scored what they saw. Priority routed the sword to the master smith. The blocked task didn't waste anyone's time. Finn self-directed into his own needs when work ran out. That's the system working.

Build this first

For any NPC, show their last scoring pass. List every task considered, the score each received, which modifiers fired. When a player asks "why is my blacksmith making horseshoes?" you open the view and see: Finn scored Horseshoes 0.7, Sword 0.3 (Smithing 8 needed, Finn has 2); Sword scored 0.3 for everyone except Aldric (busy). Explained in ten seconds. Without this tool, you'll spend hundreds of hours guessing.

Economy

Market price engine

The heart of the economy. Translates stock levels and demand pressure into current prices, updated continuously as transactions happen.

Three core responsibilities

  1. Set a price for each resource given current market conditions
  2. Absorb transactions (deposits, purchases) and update stock
  3. Track demand signals so price responds to what buyers are trying to do, not just stock

The basic supply/demand curve

price(resource) = base_price × supply_factor × demand_factor

base_price is the "normal" price per recipe (production cost + modest markup). Bread 5g, sword 80g.

supply_factor(resource):
  stock = market.stock[resource]
  normal_stock = market.normal_level[resource]
  ratio = stock / normal_stock
  return clamp(1.0 / ratio, MIN_SUPPLY_MULT, MAX_SUPPLY_MULT)

Stock at half normal → price 2x. Stock at double → price 0.5x. Clamps prevent absurdities. Reasonable: MIN=0.4, MAX=3.0.

Defining "normal stock"

Use dynamic with a slow moving average (30-day window). When a town grows, normal stock rises, prices re-baseline, economy expands smoothly.

update_normal_stock(resource):
  recent_consumption = sum(purchases in last 30 days) / 30
  market.normal_level[resource] = recent_consumption × NORMAL_DAYS_OF_STOCK

NORMAL_DAYS_OF_STOCK is a knob — 3-7 for most goods. Short for perishables, long for durables.

The demand signal problem

If you only look at stock, prices lag reality. Plague hits → everyone wants medicine → stock drains → price rises only after the crash.

Track demand pressure separately. Crucially, this includes failed buy attempts (NPCs who tried but couldn't because stock was empty or they couldn't afford it):

demand_factor(resource):
  recent_demand = sum(attempted_purchases in last N ticks)
  normal_demand = market.normal_demand[resource]
  return clamp(recent_demand / normal_demand, MIN_DEMAND, MAX_DEMAND)

This is how the "deferred demand → price spike when stock returns" from your diagrams actually works.

Price smoothing (anti-jitter)

If price recalculates on every transaction, it twitches each time a single NPC buys a loaf. Apply exponential smoothing:

update_price(resource):
  target = base_price × supply_factor × demand_factor
  current = market.price[resource]
  market.price[resource] = current × (1 - SMOOTHING) + target × SMOOTHING

With SMOOTHING = 0.15, a sudden shortage takes ~5-10 ticks to fully reflect. Gives players time to notice and react.

Price floors and ceilings

price(resource):
  raw = base_price × supply_factor × demand_factor
  return clamp(raw, market.floor[resource], market.ceiling[resource])

The ceiling is the trigger for emergency import. When a resource pins against its ceiling too long, city AI imports from other towns. The ceiling is the emergency import threshold — same concept.

How transactions hit the market

sell(seller, resource, qty):
  price_at_sale = market.price[resource]
  payout = price_at_sale × qty
  market.stock[resource] += qty
  market.transactions.append(Sale(seller, resource, qty, price_at_sale))
  seller.gold += payout
  update_price(resource)

buy(buyer, resource, qty):
  if market.stock[resource] < qty:
    market.attempted_purchases[resource] += qty  // track for demand
    return FAILED_NO_STOCK
  price_at_sale = market.price[resource]
  cost = price_at_sale × qty
  if buyer.gold < cost:
    market.attempted_purchases[resource] += qty  // wanted, couldn't afford
    return FAILED_NO_GOLD
  market.stock[resource] -= qty
  buyer.gold -= cost
  seller_payouts_queue.add(resource, qty, cost)
  update_price(resource)

Seller payouts (pro-rata)

Each seller's share of current stock determines their share of payout. Simple, feels fair, works with the abstract market pool:

settle_payout(resource, qty_sold, total_paid):
  for seller in market.sellers_for(resource):
    share = seller.stock_in_market / market.total_stock
    seller.gold += total_paid × share
    seller.stock_in_market -= qty_sold × share

Per-town markets and trade routes

Each town has its own market with independent prices. Price differentials become player opportunity: iron 5g in mining town, 15g in farming town → player hauls iron and profits. This is Guild 2's trade loop.

World:
  markets: map of TownId → Market
  trade_routes: list of TradeRoute

TradeRoute:
  from, to, distance (days), base_tariff (0.2 = 20% markup)

When Town A runs out of iron, city AI finds Town B with surplus, buys at B's price + tariff + distance, injects at premium (near ceiling). Player can undercut this by hauling themselves at cheaper markup.

Price discovery for the player

  1. Current price — always shown
  2. 7-day history sparkline — critical for decisions
  3. Inter-town comparison — only if player visited that town recently (stale data is fine)

The player's knowledge of other towns is N days old. Travel and information become valuable.

Edge cases

  • Empty market — pin price to ceiling, keep accumulating demand. First seller back gets the premium.
  • Dumping flood — stock above normal, price crashes to floor. Moving average distorts upward. Other producers scale back. Market corrects over weeks. Dumper loses money too — realistic.
  • Runaway spiral — prevent by using smoothed prices for producer decisions, adding NPC price memory, letting production latency absorb swings
  • Currency deflation — if money drains to sinks faster than it enters, inject via city AI wages, bounties, public works. Monitor total money supply over time.

Tick cadence

  • Per transaction: update stock, record demand, recalc target price
  • Per game-hour: update smoothed price toward target
  • Daily: decay old attempted_purchases, clean transaction log
  • Weekly: recalculate normal_stock, normal_demand
  • Monthly: audit money supply, adjust tax rates

Per-resource tuning

CategoryConsumptionDurabilityVolumeElasticity
Food (bread, meat)DailyPerishableHighElastic
Raw materials (iron, wood)WeeklyDurableMediumInelastic
Luxury (jewellery, fine swords)RareVery durableLowInelastic, huge markup

Each resource needs its own base_price, normal_days_of_stock, floor, ceiling, possibly own SMOOTHING. Luxuries can swing wildly; staples stay stable. Stick in a data file.

Build order

Pass 1: stock-based supply_factor only. Pass 2: add demand_factor from attempts. Pass 3: add smoothing, floors/ceilings. Pass 4: per-town markets and imports. Pass 5: tune per-resource. Each pass reveals what the next actually needs.

Presentation

Player legibility

The simulation is only as good as the player's ability to read it. Guild 2's biggest criticism was exactly this — stuff happened and you couldn't tell why. Don't repeat that mistake.

Three questions to answer at any moment

  1. What is happening right now? (state)
  2. Why did that happen? (causation)
  3. What's about to happen? (anticipation)

Layer 1: showing state

  • Worker status badges — icon per NPC: hammering, walking, idle, hungry, sleeping, fleeing
  • Workshop dashboards — inventory, active tasks, headcount, today's revenue/expenses
  • Market view — price, stock, 7-day trend sparkline, direction arrow
  • Notification log — running feed of significant events (trait gains, wage requests, quits, rivals, price ceiling hits)

Notifications must be short and actionable. "Hilda gained the Bitter trait" not "An employee has experienced a personality change based on recent conditions."

Layer 2: showing causation

The decision log per NPC is the single most important legibility tool:

Hilda — Decision log
  14:22  Claimed: Craft Sword (utility 0.82)
         Considered: Repair Tools (0.31), Craft Nails (0.54), Eat (0.40)
         Modifiers: skill_match +0.3, loyal trait +0.15, proximity +0.07
  13:45  Finished: Gather Iron (3 units)
  13:20  Claimed: Gather Iron (utility 0.61)

Doesn't need to be pretty. It just needs to exist. Build this in week one — it's free debugging for you, valuable transparency for players. Show simplified version to players (top 3 considered tasks with scores), keep verbose for debug.

  • Price history with annotations — mark why on trend lines. "Rival dumped 80 loaves" / "City bulk order" / "Stock depleted." Transforms a graph into a story.
  • Rival observation reports — after weeks of observation: "Müller bakery: medium size, prioritises volume over quality. Bought a mill last month. Employs 4 workers."
  • Trait effect tooltips — show what it's currently doing. Not just "Greedy: values money" but "Greedy: currently making Hilda 40% less likely to accept her wage."

Layer 3: showing anticipation

  • Need forecasts — "Workshop runs out of iron in ~2 days at current rate"
  • Price forecasts (soft) — directional hints only: "Iron supply trending down"
  • Employee risk indicators — warning icon on any employee likely to quit (wage dissatisfaction, trait conflicts, overworked)
  • Construction progress bars — "New mine: ready in 3 days"
  • Scheduled events — tax day approaching, commission due, weekly wage payout total

Transparency / opacity trade-off

TierExamples
Always transparentOwn business state, employee traits/skills, market prices, notifications, own finances
Semi-transparent (discovery)Rival archetypes (after weeks), other towns' prices (stale), NPC pool availability
OpaqueRivals' exact finances, rivals' decision scores, raw utility math, city AI's full tree

Principle: everything the player could theoretically observe in the game world should eventually be surfaced in UI.

Diegetic vs non-diegetic

Guild-style games benefit from mostly non-diegetic with diegetic flavour touches. Players are making business decisions — they need fast information access. But scatter diegetic touches (town crier announcing price news, NPCs gossiping about rival expansion) for texture.

Attention management

  • Red urgent alerts — immediate action needed. Interrupt. Be conservative.
  • Amber warnings — act within a day or two. Log + persistent icon.
  • Info notifications — FYI only. Log, no interruption.

If you cry wolf, players learn to ignore alerts. Red means "stop what you're doing."

The "living world" effect

Beyond charts, visualise the simulation:

  • Crowd flow — NPCs visibly walking to market when prices are good, queuing when scarce
  • Shop animations — workers at tasks, full inventory looks full, empty looks empty
  • Town mood — simple prosperity indicator affecting ambient animations
  • Rival visibility — when a rival hires an NPC, that NPC visibly walks to their workshop

When the player sees NPCs swarming the bread stall during a price crash, they understand the simulation in their gut.

Three tools to build early

  1. NPC decision log — why any NPC is doing what. Debug gold, player transparency
  2. Price trend graph with annotations — makes economy visible and teachable
  3. Notification log — player's peripheral attention, low-effort, huge impact on "feels alive"

Build these in month one and everything gets easier. Bugs found faster, tuning faster, playtesters understand faster.
